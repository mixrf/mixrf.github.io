Abstract

Recent advancements in neural rendering methods, particularly Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) and 3D Gaussian Splatting (3D-GS), have revolutionized photo-realistic novel view synthesis from multiple input images or videos. However, current approaches in both NeRF and 3D-GS frameworks typically treat point sampling and ray casting as independent processes, which are fundamental to volume rendering and alpha-blending techniques. This independence assumption restricts the effective aggregation of contextual information within subspaces, such as densities and colors in radiance fields and pixels on the image plane, resulting in synthesized images that lack fine details and smoothness. To address these limitations, we introduce MixRF, a universal framework that consists of two key components: the Radiance Field Mixer (RF-mixer) and the Color Domain Mixer (CD-mixer). These components work together to effectively aggregate and explore information from neighboring sampled points and casting rays. The RF-mixer treats sampled points as an explicit point cloud, enabling the aggregation of density and color attributes from neighboring points to better capture local geometry and appearance. Meanwhile, the CD-mixer rearranges rendered pixels on the sub-image plane, improving smoothness and recovering fine details and textures. Both mixers employ a kernel-based mixing strategy to facilitate effective and controllable attribute aggregation, ensuring a more comprehensive exploration of radiance values and pixel information. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that our MixRF framework is compatible with various radiance field-based methods, including both NeRF and 3D-GS designs. The proposed framework significantly enhances performance in both qualitative and quantitative evaluations, while maintaining computational efficiency with less than a 25% increase in computational overhead during inference.



Overview of MixRF pipeline. Our method incorporates two modules that seamlessly integrate into traditional neural rendering pipelines: the RF-mixer and CD-mixer. The RF-mixer performs a two-stage aggregation of radiance values (i.e., density and color) within a subspace. This involves aggregating sampled points at the same depth across neighboring rays (inter-ray) and adjacent points along the same ray (intra-ray), effectively fusing geometric and appearance information from surrounding points. The CD-mixer first refines rendered pixel patches with Plücker coordinates as a view consistency constraint, then mixes pixels within sub-image planes, guided by an error map derived from the comparison between the rendered and refined patches, thereby facilitating targeted texture recovery.


Overview of RF-mixer. We perform a two-stage integration process that involves mixing sample attributes (i.e., density and color) within neighboring rays (inter-ray) at the same sampling depth and among adjacent samples along the ray (intra-ray). Initially, we extract NeRF variables, including densities, colors, and their associated features $ \{\sigma, \boldsymbol{c}, \boldsymbol f_{\sigma}, \boldsymbol f_c \} $. Subsequently, we feed $ \{ \sigma, \boldsymbol f_{\sigma} \} $ and $ \{ \boldsymbol c, \boldsymbol f_c \} $ into the weight predictor $ \mathcal G $ and block extractor $ \mathcal E $, separately deriving corresponding sets of mixing weights and local blocks $ \{\boldsymbol W_{\sigma}, \boldsymbol B_{\sigma} \} $ and $ \{\boldsymbol W_c, \boldsymbol B_c \} $. This is followed by a weighted summation of elements within each set, yielding the inter-ray mixed density and color $ \{\sigma', \boldsymbol c' \} $. Analogously, the intra-ray mixed density and color $ \{\sigma'', \boldsymbol c'' \} $ are obtained from variables $ \{\sigma', \boldsymbol c', \boldsymbol f_{\sigma}, \boldsymbol f_c \} $ in the second stage. Our RF-mixer is trained end-to-end, supervised by the sum of the $L_2$ distances between the ground truth and pixels by volume rendered (VR).


Overview of CD-mixer. Initially, we refine the rendered pixel patch $ \boldsymbol C_{vr} $ by decoding concatenated features from the rendered pixel and the camera view. To ensure view consistency, we use Plucker coordinates to represent a ray $ \boldsymbol r = (\boldsymbol d, \boldsymbol p \times \boldsymbol d) $, establishing a correspondence with the pixel, where $\boldsymbol p $ and $\boldsymbol d $ are the camera position and ray direction, respectively. Subsequently, the weight predictor $\mathcal G_{\text{2D}} $ utilizes the error map between the pixel patched of the rendered $\boldsymbol{C}_{vr}$ and refined $\boldsymbol{C}_{rf}$ to predict mixing weights $\boldsymbol{W}_p$. Meanwhile, the block extractor $\mathcal E_{\text{2D}} $ converts the refined pixels into corresponding local blocks $\boldsymbol{B}_p$. The final mixed pixels are obtained through a weighted summation between $ \boldsymbol W_p $ and $ \boldsymbol B_p $.
